Reflection

This 17-week journey of learning Japanese was not a straight upward line. It involved experimentation, frustration, adjustment, and gradual improvement. At the beginning of the project, I assumed that following a well-known grammar guide would naturally lead to steady progress. However, I soon realised that simply reading explanations did not translate into meaningful comprehension.

I spent time researching different learning approaches online, including discussions on platforms such as Reddit, where learners shared their experiences with various methods. Some advocated heavy grammar study, others emphasised immersion, while others recommended strict spaced repetition systems. Initially, I tried following advice exactly as written. However, I discovered that blindly copying someone else’s method did not guarantee results for me personally.

The turning point came when I began mixing and matching strategies instead of committing rigidly to a single one. I retained useful grammar references from Tae Kim, incorporated immersion principles from the Refold approach, and eventually adopted Wagotabi as my primary structured learning tool. This combination created a system that suited my learning style: structured progression through gameplay, reinforced by contextual exposure through YouTube and music.

Ups, Downs, and Adaptation

The early phase of this project was slower and less efficient than expected. I struggled with translating grammar explanations into practical understanding. There were moments where progress felt stagnant. Instead of abandoning the goal, I reflected on what was not working and made deliberate adjustments.

After adopting Wagotabi and incorporating consistent immersion, learning became smoother and more intuitive. Rather than forcing memorisation, I was repeatedly exposed to sentence patterns and vocabulary in context. This reduced friction and increased engagement.

One important lesson I learned is that effectiveness depends less on choosing the “best” method according to others and more on choosing the method that aligns with how I personally learn.

Development of Personal Attributes

Resilience

Resilience was developed when my initial learning strategy did not produce the results I expected. Instead of giving up or assuming I lacked ability, I evaluated the process itself. Adjusting my strategy required patience and acceptance that early inefficiency is part of long-term growth. This mindset is transferable to technical projects in my IIT (AAI) studies, where debugging and iteration are normal parts of development.

Inquisitiveness

I demonstrated inquisitiveness by actively researching different learning philosophies and analysing why certain methods work for some learners. Rather than passively consuming advice, I compared approaches, experimented, and refined my system. This habit of questioning and investigating will be valuable in artificial intelligence and technology fields, where continuous learning and exploration of new tools are essential.

Effective Communication

Although I am still at a beginner level in Japanese, I have developed stronger foundational comprehension skills. I can recognise basic sentence structures, identify particles such as は and を, and understand simple polite verb forms. More importantly, I have learned how communication works structurally in another language.

This awareness strengthens my ability to think about language systems, which connects to programming and AI model design — both require understanding structured input and output patterns.

Future Application

This project has taught me more than just beginner Japanese. It has reinforced the importance of building sustainable systems, testing strategies, and iterating when necessary.

In future learning — whether technical skills, programming languages, or professional development — I will apply the same principle: research widely, experiment carefully, and refine until the system fits my workflow.

Ultimately, this journey showed me that effective learning is not about copying others perfectly. It is about understanding oneself as a learner, building a method that aligns with personal strengths, and maintaining steady progress over time.
