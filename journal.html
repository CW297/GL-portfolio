Learning Journal

This journal records my progress, challenges, strategy changes, and reflections throughout my Japanese learning journey aligned to JLPT N5. It also documents how I collected evidence (screenshots, notes, Anki reviews, and immersion activities) for my learning portfolio.

Week 1 – Planning & Goal Setting

+

This week focused on setting a clear learning goal and translating it into measurable outcomes. I decided that “learning Japanese” is too broad, so I anchored my project to JLPT N5 as a concrete target. I broke the goal into skills: kana fluency (Hiragana/Katakana), foundational grammar patterns, vocabulary building, and basic listening/reading ability.

I researched different approaches: structured grammar-first study vs. immersion-based learning. I explored Tae Kim’s Guide to understand how beginner grammar is sequenced and what “core grammar” looks like (particles, copula, basic verb forms). I also researched Anki as a retention system and learned why active recall + spaced repetition is commonly used for language learning.

What I learned: JLPT N5 is achievable when I treat it as a set of micro-skills, not one huge task. A good plan needs a main curriculum + review system + evidence capture.

Week 2 – Building a Study System

+

I refined my weekly plan and decided how I would capture learning evidence. I listed what “proof” looks like: kana practice screenshots, Anki deck stats, Wagotabi vocabulary/grammar screenshots, and short reflections explaining what I understood and what I struggled with.

I studied Anki mechanics in more detail (new cards vs. review cards, interval scheduling, and why consistency matters more than “cramming”). For grammar, I skimmed Tae Kim’s early sections and noted the concepts that would likely cause confusion later (particles, verb conjugations, and sentence order).

What I learned: A learning project becomes easier when the “system” is prepared first. Evidence capture should be done while learning, not at the end.

Week 3 – Hiragana Foundations

+

I started Hiragana using Anki and a kana reference (Uchisen). I focused on recognition and reading fluency, not just memorising a chart. I practised confusing pairs (さ/き/ち, ぬ/め/ね, れ/わ) and used repetition until recognition felt automatic.

Alongside kana, I read Tae Kim’s introduction to understand basic Japanese sentence structure. I learned that particles are essential because Japanese relies less on word order and more on markers like は/が/を to show roles in a sentence.

What I learned: Kana fluency is the “gateway skill.” Without it, everything else becomes slower because I will constantly be decoding.

Week 4 – Katakana + Early Grammar Attempts

+

I began Katakana using Anki and Uchisen charts. I paid extra attention to characters that look similar to each other or to Hiragana (シ/ツ, ソ/ン, ノ/メ, ル/レ). I also practised reading loanwords to make Katakana feel practical rather than abstract.

I attempted to start Tae Kim’s grammar more seriously (copula / basic sentence patterns). I could understand the explanation when reading, but I noticed I struggled to produce my own examples confidently. That showed me I needed more contextual reinforcement, not just reading explanations.

What I learned: Recognising Katakana improves real-world exposure (signs, menus, names). For grammar, understanding ≠ ability to use.

Week 5 – Nouns, Adjectives, and Simple Sentences

+

This week I focused on forming very simple sentences using nouns/adjectives and polite patterns. I practised identifying the topic of a sentence, and experimented with building short statements like “X is Y” and “X is not Y” in a beginner-friendly structure.

I started collecting vocabulary that appeared repeatedly in beginner materials and reinforced it using Anki. My key difficulty was that grammar study felt “dry,” and I caught myself losing momentum.

What I learned: I need a learning method that makes grammar feel usable (seeing it in action), otherwise motivation drops.

Week 6 – Verbs & Polite Form (ます)

+

I focused on verbs and polite form (ます). I learned that many beginner sentences rely on the pattern “(topic) は (object) を (verb-ます)”. This gave me a practical template to understand a lot of early Japanese.

I practised recognising verbs in dictionary form vs. polite form and began building awareness of conjugation categories. I also noted that rote memorisation of conjugation tables felt slow without context.

What I learned: The ます-form is extremely high-value for N5 comprehension. Even if I can’t conjugate perfectly yet, recognising the pattern helps me understand sentences quickly.

Week 7 – Discovering Refold & Immersion

+

While continuing grammar study, I came across the Refold language learning approach, which emphasises immersion and consistent exposure to the target language.

I learned that immersion does not mean understanding everything immediately, but rather exposing myself to Japanese content regularly so that patterns become familiar over time.

From this week onward, I began incorporating immersion alongside my structured learning. I started watching Japanese YouTube content such as vlogs and gaming videos created for Japanese audiences. I also began listening to more Japanese music on Spotify.

What I learned: Even when I cannot understand everything, consistent exposure trains listening familiarity and reduces the mental barrier to the language.

Week 8 – Discovering Wagotabi

+

After experimenting with grammar-heavy study and beginning immersion, I decided to look for a more engaging structured system. This is when I discovered Wagotabi.

Wagotabi presents Japanese learning in a game format, gradually introducing vocabulary and sentence patterns in context. I found it much easier to follow because I simply needed to boot up the game and progress naturally.

Immersion through YouTube and Spotify continued alongside this, reinforcing sounds and rhythm outside of structured gameplay.

What I learned: Engagement and structure together make learning far more sustainable.

Week 9 – Particles in Context (は / を)

+

Through Wagotabi, I encountered repeated sentence structures such as: Topic は Object を Verb ます. I began clearly understanding how は marks the topic and を marks the direct object.

For example, sentences like 「女の子はパンを買います」 became easier to parse because I could identify each grammatical role.

Immersion continued daily through Japanese YouTube videos and music. Even when comprehension was partial, I began recognising familiar words from Wagotabi appearing in spoken content.

What I learned: Seeing grammar in context repeatedly builds intuitive understanding.

Week 10 – Verb Forms & Reinforcement

+

Continued progressing through Wagotabi lessons, encountering verbs in polite form (ます) such as 買います and 食べます. I became more comfortable recognising these forms instantly.

Immersion through Spotify and YouTube remained consistent. I began noticing recurring phrases and intonation patterns even if I did not fully understand full sentences.

What I learned: Repeated exposure across multiple mediums strengthens retention and recognition speed.

Week 11 – Question Structures & Vocabulary Growth

+

This week I encountered question structures ending in か and began recognising how yes/no questions are formed.

Vocabulary expanded further, including words like 肉, 魚, 弁当, and パン. Seeing these words repeatedly in sentences improved recall.

Immersion remained ongoing. I watched Japanese vlogs and gaming videos aimed at native speakers, allowing me to hear natural conversational rhythm.

What I learned: Vocabulary retention improves when reinforced in structured lessons and heard in real-life content.

Week 12 – Increased Reading Fluency

+

Continued progressing through Wagotabi. By this point, Hiragana and Katakana recognition became significantly faster.

I no longer needed to decode every character slowly, which allowed me to focus more on understanding sentence meaning.

Immersion continued consistently through music and YouTube, further increasing familiarity with pronunciation patterns.

What I learned: Automatic recognition reduces cognitive load and improves comprehension speed.

Week 13 – Pattern Recognition

+

Began recognising sentence structures more intuitively instead of translating word-by-word.

Repeated exposure to patterns like Topic + は + Object + を + Verb allowed me to understand beginner sentences more fluidly.

Immersion remained part of my routine. I increasingly noticed words from Wagotabi appearing in songs and spoken content.

What I learned: Recognition becomes automatic when exposure is frequent and varied.

Week 14 – Balancing Learning & Portfolio

+

Began building my e-portfolio website. Although Japanese study time reduced slightly, I continued progressing through Wagotabi.

Immersion through YouTube and Spotify remained consistent. Even with reduced structured study time, exposure to Japanese continued daily.

What I learned: Consistency does not require large time blocks. Even moderate exposure maintains progress.

Week 15 – Slower but Continuous Progress

+

Continued Wagotabi progression at a slightly slower pace due to portfolio commitments.

Immersion remained consistent. I continued listening to Japanese music and watching native content, maintaining exposure to real spoken language.

What I learned: Long-term language growth depends on sustainability, not speed.

Week 16 – Strengthening Familiarity

+

Continued structured progression in Wagotabi, encountering cumulative vocabulary and grammar patterns.

Immersion continued consistently. Familiar words became easier to catch in spoken content.

What I learned: Familiarity grows gradually through repetition across different contexts.

Week 17 – Continued Learning with Reduced Time

+

While finalising my portfolio, Japanese learning continued at a slower pace. I still progressed in Wagotabi lessons, encountering new words and reinforcing existing grammar.

Immersion remained consistent up to this week, through Japanese music and YouTube content.

What I learned: Even when time is limited, maintaining engagement with the language prevents regression and supports long-term growth.
